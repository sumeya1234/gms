Abstract

Vehicle repair and maintenance services are essential for ensuring safe and reliable transportation. However, many garages still rely on manual and traditional management methods, which limit efficiency and service transparency. With the increasing use of mobile and web technologies, there is a strong need for digital solutions that can improve communication, service quality, and operational efficiency in garage management.

Customers often face difficulties in finding reliable garages, accessing clear service information, experiencing long waiting times, and tracking repair progress. Garage administrators struggle with poor task coordination, manual record keeping, inventory management issues, and inefficient payment handling. Mechanics typically rely on informal communication, which increases delays and errors. These challenges reduce service quality, efficiency, and customer trust.

This project proposes a garage management system developed using modern web and mobile technologies. The customer and mechanic mobile applications are built using React Native, while the administrator web dashboard is developed with ReactJS. A MySQL database is used for secure and structured data storage. The system integrates Chapa as a digital payment gateway to support secure cashless transactions. The platform includes role-based access control and real-time updates, allowing customers to track services, administrators to manage operations, and mechanics to update job status efficiently.

The proposed garage management system replaces manual processes with a unified digital platform. It improves communication, enhances operational efficiency, and increases transparency among customers, administrators, and mechanics. By integrating mobile applications, web technologies, and digital payments, the system provides better service delivery and an improved user experience for all stakeholders.





Chapter One

Introduction and Background

1.1. Introduction

1.1.1. Overview of the topic

The automotive service sector plays a crucial role in supporting transportation systems and enabling economic productivity. However, in many regions, particularly in developing countries, vehicle repair and maintenance services remain largely unstructured, unreliable, and predominantly manual. Customers frequently struggle to locate credible garages, mechanics face communication challenges, and admins rely on outdated processes that hinder efficiency. These issues contribute to long waiting times, inconsistent pricing, poor service documentation, and a general lack of transparency and accountability within the sector. As the demand for professional vehicle repair continues to rise, the traditional system characterized by informal networks and limited technological integration fails to meet the expectations of modern consumers. This project addresses the pressing need to digitalize garage operations by introducing an integrated technological solution that improves communication, operational coordination, and service quality in the automotive repair ecosystem.

1.1.2. Description of the project

This project, titled garage management system aims to design and develop an innovative system that streamlines the entire workflow of vehicle repair services. The proposed system consists of multiple coordinated modules: a customer-facing android application, a web-based garage management dashboard, a mechanic mobile application, and an administrative portal for super admins. Through these interconnected components, the platform facilitates service booking, real-time repair tracking, task assignment, inventory management, and secure digital payments using Chapa. Additional features such as emergency request handling with live location sharing, customer reviews and ratings, multilingual support, and a real-time notification system enhance the user experience and bring greater transparency to the sector. By integrating these functionalities into a single platform, the project aims to modernize service delivery and ensure smooth communication among all stakeholders: customers, mechanics, admins, and super administrators.

1.1.3. Solution to be explored

The project proposes a technologically advanced solution that combines mobile computing, web technologies, geolocation services, and digital financial systems to address inefficiencies in traditional garage service operations. At its core, the system enables real-time communication that allows garages to receive and respond to customer service requests promptly, assign mechanics efficiently, and update repair statuses dynamically.

The customer application includes a GPS-enabled garage discovery feature that displays nearby repair centers along with their available services, pricing information, and customer ratings. The mechanic interface streamlines workflow by allowing mechanics to submit diagnostic results, update repair progress, and confirm job completion in real time. In addition, integrated inventory management tools help garages maintain accurate spare-part records and generate automated alerts when stock levels are low.

The system also integrates a digital payment solution using the Chapa payment. After a service is completed and approved by the admin, customers can securely make payments through Chapa. The system verifies the payment status, ensures transaction integrity, and automatically generates a digital receipt before marking the service as completed. To enhance accountability and regulation, an administrative portal enables supervisory Super to monitor garage performance, manage garage accounts, and address customer complaints.

By integrating all these components into a single platform, the proposed system provides a centralized, secure, and real-time solution that transforms traditional garage services into a more organized, transparent, and efficient digital experience.

1.1.4. Importance of the project

This project is important because it changes the way vehicle repairs are handled, moving away from old, manual ways of doing things that often cause confusion and delays. For people who own cars, this project is a huge help because it removes the stress of trying to find a trustworthy mechanic, especially during a breakdown, by using GPS to show nearby garages and giving clear prices upfront. It acts as a real safety net since drivers can share their exact location when they are stuck on the road, making sure help arrives quickly through an emergency assistance feature.

For the garage owners and workers, the system is a game-changer because it handles the messy paperwork, tracks spare parts automatically, and helps organize who is working on what. This prevents mistakes like losing records or running out of parts, and it allows mechanics to stay focused on the actual repairs. It also makes technology easier to use for everyone so anyone can benefit from it regardless of their technical skills.

Furthermore, the project is essential because it provides a centralized digital platform to manage and monitor garage operations. The system enables garages to register and operate within the platform, while administrators oversee approvals, monitor performance, and handle customer complaints fairly.

By bringing customers, mechanics, and administrators into a single digital space, the project promotes accountability through real-time service updates, transparent records, and customer reviews. Ultimately, this project is important because it builds trust and reliability between garages and customers, making the vehicle repair process smoother, safer, and more professional for the entire community.

1.2.3. Organizational structure 

A typical garage follows a simple hierarchical structure that defines roles and responsibilities:

Admin - The admin oversees the overall operation of the garage. This role includes approving service requests, assigning mechanics to jobs, managing inventory, handling payments, and resolving customer complaints.

Mechanics - Mechanics are responsible for diagnosing vehicle problems, performing repairs, updating job progress, and reporting spare-part usage to the admin.

Customers - Customers initiate service requests, provide vehicle information, make payments, and receive completed services.

Super admin - registers garage, service standards, and complaint handling to ensure compliance and quality.

Information Flow within the garage

In the existing system, information flow is mostly informal and manual. Customers communicate service requests verbally or through phone calls. Admins then relay instructions to mechanics verbally, and updates are shared inconsistently. Inventory and payment records are often kept on paper, making tracking and reporting difficult.

The proposed garage management system improves this flow by digitizing communication. Customer requests move directly to the admin through the system, mechanics receive clear task assignments digitally, and service updates, inventory usage, and payments are recorded in real time. Super admin can access summarized information for monitoring and decision-making. This structured information flow ensures transparency, accuracy, and efficient coordination among all stakeholders.

1.5. Statement of the problem

Vehicle repair garages play an essential role in supporting transportation and daily economic activities. However, many garages still operate using traditional and manual systems that are inefficient and poorly organized. These practices create several challenges that affect customers, managers, mechanics, and supervising bodies.

One major problem is the lack of access to reliable and timely service information. Customers often struggle to find trustworthy garages, understand available services, or receive clear pricing details. Once a vehicle is submitted for repair, customers usually have no way of tracking progress, which leads to uncertainty, repeated inquiries, and dissatisfaction.

Another significant issue is the communication gap between stakeholders. In most garages, communication depends on phone calls, face-to-face conversations, or verbal instructions. Service requests, task assignments, and progress updates are not systematically recorded, resulting in delays, misunderstandings, and poor coordination between admins and mechanics.

Inventory and resource management also remains a serious challenge. Spare parts are commonly tracked manually, making it difficult to know stock levels accurately. This often leads to unexpected shortages, overstocking, delayed repairs, and increased operational costs.

In addition, payment and transaction handling is inefficient in many garages. Payments are frequently cash-based or poorly documented, which reduces transparency and increases the risk of errors, disputes, and financial mismanagement.

Finally, garages lack effective mechanisms for handling emergency service requests. In urgent situations, there is no structured way to identify nearby garages, share customer locations, or prioritize emergency repairs, resulting in slow response times and customer frustration.

These interconnected problems highlight the need for a centralized, technology-driven solution that improves communication, transparency, efficiency, and service quality in garage operations.

1.11. Limitation of the project

The implementation of the Garage Management System is subject to several constraints that may influence its operational efficiency and widespread adoption. Technically, the system’s performance—specifically regarding real-time service tracking and automated reporting—is contingent upon available computational resources, which may impact responsiveness under heavy loads. Furthermore, the transition to a digital platform presents a human factor challenge; users with varying levels of technical proficiency may require structured training to utilize the system effectively.

The scope of the application is further narrowed by its platform and connectivity requirements. Currently, mobile access is restricted to the Android operating system, and the necessity for a continuous internet connection may limit the system's utility in environments with unstable network infrastructure. Additionally, as this project was developed within an academic context, it lacks exposure to a high-volume production environment, and its reporting accuracy is based on simulated data rather than extensive real-world datasets. Finally, the system’s verification process is limited to internal document-based validation and does not extend to formal integration with government databases for legal authentication.

1.12. Risks, assumptions, and constraints

The development and implementation of the garage management system may encounter various risks that could affect system performance and adoption

Risks

Data Security and Privacy Risk:  The system stores sensitive customer, vehicle, and financial information, which may be exposed to unauthorized access or data breaches.

Risk Handling Strategy:  Secure user authentication, role-based access control, encrypted data transmission, and regular data backups will be used to protect data confidentiality and integrity.

System Failure and Downtime Risk: Technical issues such as software errors, server failures, or unexpected crashes may interrupt system availability.

Risk Handling Strategy: The system will be developed using a modular design, undergo thorough testing, and include backup and recovery mechanisms to ensure system stability and continuity.

User Adoption Risk: Some users, including garage staff and customers, may resist using the system due to limited technical skills or familiarity with digital platforms.

Risk Handling Strategy: A simple and user-friendly interface will be designed, supported by clear user guidance to encourage adoption.

Assumptions

Users (customers, admins, mechanics, and super admin) will have internet access and compatible devices.

Admins and mechanics will actively engage with the system to ensure timely service updates and inventory management.

Super admin will oversee the platform to maintain operational compliance and quality standards.





Constraints

The project operates under limited time and budget, restricting the scope of advanced features and cross-platform support.

Legal and regulatory requirements for handling customer and financial data constrain flexibility in system design and implementation.

The platform is initially restricted to Android devices and web dashboards, limiting iOS mobile adoption in the first phase.

2.1. Existing system

2.1.1. Description of the existing system

Currently, vehicle repair garages in Ethiopia and many other regions largely rely on manual, paper-based operations or partially digitized systems to manage their services. For example:

- Manual service logs: Garages record customer information, vehicle details, and service history in physical registers, making tracking and retrieval cumbersome.

- Communication methods: Customers contact garages via phone calls or in-person visits to request services or inquire about progress.

- Inventory tracking: Spare parts are often tracked manually, leading to stock discrepancies and delayed restocking.

- Payment systems: Payments are primarily cash-based, which increases the risk of errors and reduces transaction transparency.

These existing systems are functional at a basic level but are inefficient, error-prone, and lack real-time updates, which affects both customer satisfaction and garage operations.

2.1.2. Problems of the existing system

The current garage management processes face several challenges that hinder operational efficiency and customer service quality. The major problems include:

Lack of integration: Customer management, mechanic assignments, inventory, and payment systems operate independently, causing fragmentation and inefficiencies.

Limited real-time updates: Customers and admins cannot track service progress in real time, leading to uncertainty and delays.

Inefficient task assignment: Mechanics often rely on verbal or manual instructions, which can result in miscommunication and delayed repairs.

Inventory management issues: Manual tracking leads to inaccurate stock levels, unplanned shortages, and overstocking.

Payment inefficiencies: Cash-only systems or untracked digital payments increase errors and reduce financial transparency.

Neglect of customer experience: The existing system lacks features such as GPS-based garage search, service ratings, emergency assistance, or notification systems.

2.1.3. Players in the existing system

The key stakeholders involved in the existing garage system include:

- Customers: Individuals seeking vehicle repair and maintenance services.

- Managers: Responsible for coordinating mechanics, service schedules, and inventory.

- Mechanics: Perform vehicle repairs and maintenance tasks.

2.1.4. Bottlenecks Using the PIECES Framework

The PIECES framework can be used to evaluate the limitations of the current garage systems:

Performance: Slow service due to manual processes and lack of real-time tracking reduces efficiency and customer satisfaction.

Information / Input & Output: Redundant data entry for service records and inventory, coupled with inaccurate reporting, creates operational inefficiencies.

Control / Security: Physical records and cash payments are prone to errors, theft, and unauthorized access.

Efficiency: Fragmented systems force admins, mechanics, and customers to juggle multiple processes, wasting time and resources.

Services / Functionality: Limited features restrict customer engagement, such as the inability to rate services, request emergency assistance, or track repair progress digitally.

Other / Scalability: Current methods cannot easily scale to accommodate multiple garages or higher customer volumes.

2.2. Business rules for proposed system

BR01 – User eligibility and registration

- All users (customers, managers, mechanics, and super administrators) must be registered before participating in any garage-related activity.

- Admins are required to submit valid verification documents and obtain super admin approval before offering services through the platform.

BR02 – Roles and access control

- Access to data and operations is strictly determined by user roles.

- Customers can access only their personal, vehicle, and service-related information.

- Mechanics are restricted to viewing and updating tasks assigned to them.

- Admins oversee services, mechanics, inventory, and garage operations under their control.

- Super administrators have oversight access for monitoring, auditing, and governance purposes.

BR03 – Service requests and job assignment

- Service requests can only be created for vehicles that are registered under a customer’s account.

- Admins are responsible for approving or rejecting all service requests.

- A mechanic may be assigned to only up to 5 active service tasks at a time to ensure service quality and safety.

BR04 – Inventory control policies

- Spare parts inventory is managed exclusively by Admins.

- Mechanics must request approval before using spare parts when stock availability is limited or insufficient.

- Admins must ensure that inventory levels are sufficient to prevent service disruption due to stock shortages.

BR05 – Emergency service handling

- Emergency service requests are treated with higher priority than regular service requests.

- Admins are responsible for ensuring immediate response by assigning available resources.

BR06 – Payment and service completion

- A service is considered complete only after payment has been confirmed.

- All payment transactions must be recorded and retained for accountability, reporting, and auditing purposes.

BR07 – Feedback and performance oversight

- Customers are allowed to provide feedback and ratings only after a service has been completed.

- Admins and super administrators can review performance data to evaluate service quality and operational efficiency.

2.3. Proposed system

2.3.1. Proposed system description

The proposed system garage management system is designed to address the inefficiencies of current garage operations, which are often manual or fragmented across separate tools. These limitations lead to delays in service delivery, poor tracking of service progress, inventory issues, and limited accountability. The GMS provides an integrated digital platform that connects customers, admins, mechanics, and super administrators in a single workflow.

The system is built around role-based access, ensuring that each user interacts only with features relevant to their responsibilities. By automating service requests, task assignment, inventory tracking, and reporting, the system improves operational efficiency, enhances transparency, and ensures consistent enforcement of business policies.

2.3.2 Key components of the system

1. Customer application

Customers can register and manage their profiles, add multiple vehicles, and submit service requests. The app allows searching for nearby garages using GPS, viewing services and prices, and tracking service progress in real time. Emergency requests are prioritized, with optional live vehicle location sharing. Payments are handled securely, linking completion confirmation with service records.

2. Admin web dashboard

Admins can review, approve, or reject service requests, assign tasks to mechanics, and monitor service progress. The dashboard supports inventory management, including tracking spare parts and monitoring usage. It also provides summaries of garage performance, such as service volume, revenue trends, mechanic workload, and customer feedback, helping admins make informed decisions.

Mechanic mobile app

Mechanics receive task assignments with detailed service instructions. They update task status as work progresses and log spare parts usage, providing real-time visibility for both managers and customers. This interface focuses on task execution and reporting, limiting access to only relevant system features.

4. Super admin Portal

It provides oversight across all garages, approves or suspends garage accounts, monitors complaints, reviews performance metrics, and generates system-wide analytics.

2.3.3. System scope and alignment	

The GMS centralizes garage operations while enforcing business rules such as approval workflows, emergency prioritization, and payment verification. The system is technically feasible for implementation using standard web and mobile technologies. It aligns with business rules and functional requirements, ensuring that each actor can perform their responsibilities efficiently while maintaining data integrity and accountability.

The design phase of the garage management system (GMS) is a critical step in transforming conceptual requirements into a fully functional platform that optimizes garage operations and enhances customer satisfaction. System design acts as the bridge between identifying user needs, operational requirements, and technical specifications, and implementing a reliable, user-friendly solution. This chapter outlines the methodologies, tools, and processes employed to ensure the system is efficient, accessible, and scalable for admins, mechanics, and customers.

To create a robust and intuitive system, the design phase employed user-centered design principles, iterative development cycles, and stakeholder feedback. Detailed wireframes and interactive prototypes were developed using tools such as Figma to visualize key interfaces including service booking, vehicle management, and inventory tracking. Documentation and collaborative work were managed using Microsoft Word, Google Docs, and Notion, ensuring consistent version control. Additionally, tools like Edrawmax and Draw.io were used to design system architecture diagrams, workflow charts, and process flows, helping to map out interactions between customers, garage staff, and administrative functions. These methodologies and tools were selected to balance usability, performance, and operational efficiency, ensuring the system meets the practical needs of garages while providing a seamless digital experience for users.

3.2. Purpose of the System

The primary purpose of the garage management system is to provide a comprehensive, automated platform that streamlines garage operations and improves the customer experience. The system addresses multiple dimensions of garage management, including service scheduling, resource allocation, vehicle tracking, and customer communication. Its purpose can be categorized into operational, Managerial, and customer-focused support.

Operational SupportThe system enhances day-to-day garage operations by enabling staff to efficiently manage service requests, track ongoing repairs, and maintain inventory records. Mechanics can view assigned tasks, update progress in real time, and receive notifications for urgent repairs or service deadlines. The platform also includes tools for managing spare parts, service history, and equipment availability, helping garages operate smoothly and minimize downtime.

Managerial SupportAdmins can access dashboards that provide insights into performance metrics, such as revenue, completed services, mechanic efficiency, and inventory levels. The system facilitates decision-making by providing real-time analytics, automated reports, and predictive insights to optimize resource allocation and reduce operational bottlenecks. Additionally, admins can oversee staff scheduling, approve service requests, and maintain overall workflow efficiency through a centralized interface.

Customer SupportThe system improves the customer experience by offering an intuitive platform for booking vehicle services, tracking repair status, and receiving notifications for service completion. Customers can view their service history, make payments online, and submit service-related inquiries through the system.. Multi-language support ensures accessibility for diverse user groups, while integrated feedback tools allow customers to provide input on service quality, enabling continuous improvement.

By addressing these purposes, the system aims to enhance the operational efficiency of garages, provide transparent communication with customers, and facilitate better resource management. The GMS integrates all aspects of garage management into a single platform, offering a scalable, reliable, and user-friendly solution that modernizes traditional garage operations while meeting the needs of both staff and customers.

3.3. Design Goals

The garage management system (GMS) is designed with key goals of scalability, security, usability, and efficiency to ensure seamless functionality, adaptability, and long-term reliability for both garage staff and customers.

ScalabilityThe system is built to handle an increasing number of users, including multiple garages, mechanics, and customers, without performance degradation. A modular architecture allows easy expansion to add new features such as advanced inventory management, multiple branches, or loyalty programs. Cloud-based infrastructure ensures secure and reliable data storage, enabling the system to grow alongside the business.

SecurityData security and privacy are central to the system design. Sensitive information, such as customer details, vehicle records, and service histories, is protected through encryption and secure authentication protocols. Access control mechanisms ensure only authorized users can modify critical data. Regular system updates, security patches, and monitoring prevent unauthorized access and potential cyber threats, safeguarding the integrity of both operational and customer data.

UsabilityThe system is designed to be intuitive and accessible, minimizing the learning curve for garage staff and customers. Interfaces are user-friendly, with clear navigation, real-time notifications, and interactive dashboards. Cross-platform compatibility ensures smooth access from smartphones, tablets, and desktops, while responsive design principles guarantee consistent performance across devices. User testing and feedback loops inform iterative improvements, enhancing overall user satisfaction.

EfficiencyOperational efficiency is a primary goal, achieved through optimized workflows for service scheduling, vehicle tracking, and inventory management. Real-time updates allow mechanics and admins to monitor progress, allocate resources effectively, and respond quickly to urgent requests. Automated notifications, reporting dashboards, and data analytics streamline decision-making, while proactive maintenance and monitoring minimize system downtime and operational disruptions.

By adhering to these design goals, the GMS provides a robust, adaptable, and efficient solution that enhances garage operations, improves customer experience, and ensures secure, reliable management of all services and resources.

3.4. Architectural Design

The architectural design of the garage management system (GMS) follows a layered architecture approach, which separates the system into distinct layers based on responsibility. This design improves maintainability, scalability, and reliability by ensuring that changes in one layer do not directly affect others. Each layer communicates with the adjacent layer through well-defined interfaces, enabling clear data flow and controlled interaction across the system.

Presentation Layer

The presentation layer represents the user interface of the system and is responsible for all user interactions. It provides web-based and mobile-accessible interfaces for customers, admins, mechanics, and super administrators. Through this layer, users can register and authenticate, submit service requests, manage appointments, track service progress, view notifications, and access relevant information based on their roles. The presentation layer does not process business logic directly; instead, it communicates with the backend application services through secure API requests and responses, ensuring data integrity and privacy.

Application Layer

The application layer consists of the backend application services, which act as the core processing unit of the system. This layer handles all business logic and system operations, including authentication and role-based access control, service workflow management, vehicle tracking, inventory updates, payment processing, and notification handling. Requests received from the presentation layer are validated, processed, and routed appropriately within this layer. By centralizing business logic in the application layer, the system ensures consistent behavior, secure processing of sensitive data, and efficient coordination between different functional operations.

Data Layer

The data layer is responsible for data storage and retrieval through a database management system. It stores persistent system data such as user profiles, vehicle information, service records, inventory details, payment transactions, and historical logs. The application layer interacts with the data layer to read and write information as required, ensuring data consistency and reliability. This separation allows the database structure to evolve without impacting user interfaces or business logic directly.

Cloud Infrastructure Layer

The cloud infrastructure layer provides the underlying computing and storage environment required to run the system reliably. It supports database hosting, application deployment, data backups, and replication mechanisms to ensure high availability and fault tolerance. This layer enables scalability by allowing system resources to be adjusted based on user demand and ensures continuous system operation through backup and recovery mechanisms. Security controls at the infrastructure level further protect system data and services from unauthorized access or failures.

Overall architectural interaction

In this layered architecture, user actions originate at the presentation layer and are transmitted to the application layer via secure APIs. The application layer processes these requests and interacts with the data layer to store or retrieve required information. The cloud infrastructure supports all layers by providing scalable resources, backup services, and reliable system availability. This structured separation of concerns results in a robust, adaptable, and efficient system architecture that supports current garage operations while allowing future enhancements without significant redesign.

3.4.1. Subsystem Decomposition

1. User Interface Subsystem

The User Interface Subsystem serves as the primary interaction layer of the garage management system. It allows customers, admins, mechanics, and super administrators to access core system functions, including user registration and login, service request submission, viewing task assignments, monitoring inventory, checking payment status, and submitting feedback.

The interface is designed to operate smoothly across both mobile and web platforms, ensuring accessibility for users in different working environments and with varying levels of technical experience. To protect user information during system interactions, all communication between the interface and backend services is secured using HTTPS.

2. Authentication and user management subsystem

The Authentication and User Management Subsystem handles user authentication, authorization, and role-based access control within the system. It ensures that only registered and verified users can access the platform and that each user is limited to actions appropriate to their assigned role.

For example, customers are allowed to request services, mechanics can view and update assigned tasks, and admins can approve service requests and manage inventory. The subsystem also manages user sessions and secures passwords using reliable protection mechanisms, providing controlled and dependable access to the system.

3. Service management subsystem

The Service management subsystem manages all service-related activities within the system. It supports the full service lifecycle, including service request submission by customers, approval or rejection by Admins, mechanic assignment, service status updates, and completion tracking.

By enforcing a clear and structured workflow from service initiation to completion, the subsystem helps reduce delays, avoid task conflicts, and improve overall operational efficiency within the garage.

4. Inventory and payment management subsystem

This subsystem handles spare parts inventory and service payments. Admins can easily update inventory levels, pricing, and availability, while the system automatically generates alerts for low-stock items. The payment component supports transactions via Chapa and cash confirmation, ensuring that services are marked as completed only after successful payment verification. All inventory and payment records are securely stored for reporting and auditing purposes.

5. Cloud infrastructure and database subsystem

The cloud infrastructure and database subsystem provide the backbone for data storage, system availability, and scalability. It maintains user profiles, vehicle information, service records, inventory data, payment history, and feedback. Operating in a cloud-based environment, it ensures reliable performance, real-time data access, automated backups, and robust recovery mechanisms, supporting uninterrupted garage operations.

Integration of subsystems

Each subsystem operates independently while remaining smoothly integrated to ensure smooth overall functionality. The user interface subsystem routes user requests to the authentication and user management subsystem for validation before directing them to the Service Management or Inventory and Payment Subsystems. All processed data is securely stored and retrieved via the Cloud Infrastructure and Database Subsystem. This modular integration enables efficient communication between subsystems, supports scalability, and simplifies maintenance and future enhancements.

3.6. Database design

The database design of the garage management system is structured to ensure efficient data storage, integrity, scalability, and secure access. A relational database model is adopted, following normalization principles to minimize redundancy and maintain consistency. The system supports multiple garages, customers, mechanics, admins, and system administrators while enforcing strict role-based responsibilities.

3.6.1 User and role modeling

The system applies a disjoint and total specialization of users. Every user belongs to exactly one role, and no user can have multiple roles simultaneously. Common user information is stored in a single parent entity, while role-specific data is stored in separate child entities.

Users (Parent entity)

Attributes: UserID (Primary Key), FullName, Email, PhoneNumber, PasswordHash, Status, CreatedAt

Description: Stores authentication and profile information common to all users.

Role-Specific entities 

Customers

Attributes: UserID (Primary Key, Foreign Key → Users)

Description: Represents system users who request garage services and own vehicles.

Mechanics

Attributes: UserID (Primary Key, Foreign Key → Users), GarageID (Foreign Key)

Description:Represents mechanics assigned to garages and responsible for service execution.

Admins

Attributes: UserID (Primary Key, Foreign Key → Users), GarageID (Foreign Key)

Description: Represents admins responsible for overseeing operations.

Super admins

Attributes:UserID (Primary Key, Foreign Key → Users)

Description:Represents system administrators with full system privileges.

3.6.2 Core operational entities

Garages

Attributes: GarageID (Primary Key), GarageName, Location, ContactNumber, Status, AdminID (Foreign Key → Admins.UserID)

Description: Represents registered garages managed by a designated admin.

Vehicles

Attributes: VehicleID (Primary Key), PlateNumber, VehicleType, Model, CustomerID (Foreign Key → Customers.UserID)

Description: Represents customer-owned vehicles that can be serviced by garages.

ServiceRequests

Attributes: RequestID (Primary Key), ServiceType, Description, RequestDate, Status, VehicleID (Foreign Key), GarageID (Foreign Key)

Description: Represents service requests submitted for vehicle maintenance or repair.

MechanicAssignments

Attributes: AssignmentID (Primary Key), RequestID (Foreign Key), MechanicID (Foreign Key → Mechanics.UserID), AssignedDate, CompletionDate, Status

Description: Manages the assignment of mechanics to service requests.

Inventory

Attributes: ItemID (Primary Key), ItemName, Quantity, UnitPrice, GarageID (Foreign Key)

Description: Represents spare parts and materials used during vehicle repairs.

Payments

Attributes: PaymentID (Primary Key), Amount, PaymentMethod (Chapa/Cash), PaymentStatus, PaymentDate, RequestID (Foreign Key)

Description: Stores payment transactions associated with completed service requests.

Reviews

Attributes: ReviewID (Primary Key), Rating, Comment, ReviewDate, CustomerID (Foreign Key → Customers.UserID), GarageID (Foreign Key)

Description: Stores customer feedback for garages after service completion.

3.6.3 Normalization and integrity

Normalization

The database schema is designed in accordance with Third Normal Form (3NF) to reduce redundancy and maintain consistent functional dependencies. Shared user attributes are stored centrally in the Users table, while role-specific information is separated into specialized entities. This structure improves data consistency and simplifies future maintenance.

Referential integrity

Referential integrity is enforced through foreign key constraints across related tables. These constraints ensure that relationships between entities remain valid and prevent the creation of orphan records, preserving the accuracy and reliability of the database.

3.6.4 Indexing strategy

To improve query efficiency and overall system responsiveness, indexes are applied to frequently accessed attributes. These include:

UserID

GarageID

RequestID

VehicleID

Indexing these attributes reduces query execution time, particularly for operations involving service tracking, user management, and vehicle records.

3.6.5 Security and access control

Security considerations are integrated into the database design to protect sensitive information and restrict unauthorized access. User passwords are stored using secure hashing techniques to prevent exposure of credentials.

Role-based access control is applied to ensure that users can only perform actions permitted by their assigned roles. Sensitive operations, such as inventory updates and administrative actions, are further restricted based on user privileges, strengthening overall system security.

3.6.6 Backup, recovery, and scalability

To ensure data reliability and continuity, automated daily backups are maintained using secure off-site storage. These backups support data recovery in the event of system failure, data corruption, or accidental loss.

Periodic recovery testing is conducted to verify backup effectiveness and system readiness. The database design also supports both horizontal and vertical scaling, allowing the system to grow in terms of users, garages, and service volume without requiring major schema changes.
